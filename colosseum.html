The Colosseum is the largest amphitheatre in Roman history, which was built in the year 72 CE, by Roman emperor Vespasian as a gift to the Roman population. The structure measured about 189 by 156 metres (or 620 x 513 feet). The amphitheatre was opened by Roman emperor Titus, who hosted gladiatorial games in the Colosseum for a hundred days, but resulted in the loss of about 5,000 animals. Emperors would traditionally attend the games. The main purpose of the amphitheatre is to entertain the people of Rome.

Gladiator battles sometimes include wild animals e.g. elephants and tigers, wherein the Colosseum, it is common to have animal fights in it. Over time, interest in the games declined, as gladiator battles were banned in 404 CE by emperor Honorius. Eventually, earthquakes in the region resulted in the deterioration of the structure of the Colosseum. By the twentieth century, nearly two-thirds of the original building was destroyed. The Colosseum was only restored in the 1990s.
