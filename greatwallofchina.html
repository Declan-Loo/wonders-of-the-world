The Great Wall of China is a series of ancient walls and fortifications with a total length of over 13,000 miles (21,000 kilometres). It was erected to fortify against nomadic invaders from the north in the third century BC. It is known to be the longest man-made structure in the world. The wall isn't just a normal wall but it consists of defensive fortifications, for example, barracks, beacon towers, garrison stations, and fortresses that are constructed alongside with the wall, for a complex defensive network of interconnected structures.
 
The wall took 2,000 years to build with the first existence of the wall being in the Spring and Autumn Period (770-476 BC) and the Warring States Period (475-221 BC), when China consisted of small states with princes ruling over them. The princes commissioned for independent walls to be built to protect their own states. The earliest was probably built between the states of Lu and Qi around 650 BC, which later became part of the Chu State Wall in modern-day Nanjing, Jiangsu province. However, it was emperor Qin Shihuang (during the Qin dynasty during 221 BC to 206 BC) who unified China, that ordered for the walls to be connected together to protect from threats from the north by Mongol rulers. The wall continued to be strengthened and lengthened during the Han Dynasty, but construction halted during the Yuan Dynasty, when the Mongols were controlling China. Eventually, the wall was rebuilt during the Ming Dynasty (1368 - 1644), and stopped after the fall of that era. Construction on the wall stopped during the whole of the Qing Dynasty.

The wall began in the east at the Shanhai Pass, which is 305km east of Beijing), and ends in the west at the Gobi Desert, at Jiayu Pass.
