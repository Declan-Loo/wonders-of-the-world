For hundreds of years, the ancient city of Petra remained "lost" to the Western world. Located in the Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan's southwestern corner, amid rough desert canyons and mountains. Between 400 B.C. through A.D. 106, Petra was the capital of the Nabataean empire, which prospered on frankincense, myrrh, and spice commerce. After being captured by the Roman Empire, the city thrived until a massive earthquake in 363 AD devastated much of the city in the 4th century AD. Petra appears to have been entirely deserted by the middle of the seventh century, and it was then lost to all but local Bedouin. In 1812, Johannes Burckhardt, a Swiss explorer, set out to 'rediscover' Petra, who successfully did, attracting visitors from the West, and continues to do so today.

It was known to be the red rose city due to cliff faces that the city was carved directly into. Over 10,000 years of human settlement and land use can be traced at Petra as well. Eventually, this is why Petra is Jordan's most remarkable tourist attraction.
